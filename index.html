Option Pricing with Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

In this project, I developed a physics-informed neural network to solve the Black-Scholes option pricing model for a single asset. This innovative approach combines the power of neural networks with the constraints of financial mathematical equations, offering a new perspective on option pricing methodologies.

What are PINNs?

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) are a class of deep learning models that incorporate physical laws or mathematical equations into the learning process. In the context of finance, "physics" refers to the governing equations of financial models. PINNs are designed to solve supervised learning tasks while respecting these underlying mathematical principles, allowing for solutions that are both data-driven and theory-consistent.

The Black-Scholes Partial Differential Equation

The core of the Black-Scholes model is a partial differential equation (PDE) that describes the behavior of option prices. For a European call option, this PDE is given by:

\[ \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2S^2\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + rS\frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0 \]

Where:

  • $V(S,t)$ is the option price as a function of the underlying asset price $S$ and time $t$
  • $\sigma$ is the volatility of the underlying asset
  • $r$ is the risk-free interest rate
  • $\frac{\partial V}{\partial t}$ represents the rate of change of the option price with respect to time
  • $\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}$ and $\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}$ are the first and second partial derivatives of $V$ with respect to $S$, also known as the option's Delta and Gamma, respectively

PINN Implementation for Option Pricing

In my implementation, I used a PINN to solve the Black-Scholes PDE. The neural network was trained to satisfy both the PDE and the initial/boundary conditions of the option pricing problem. This approach offered several advantages:

  • Accurate pricing of call options for a single asset
  • Faster computation compared to traditional numerical methods, especially for complex scenarios
  • The ability to handle the non-linear nature of the Black-Scholes equation directly within the neural network architecture

Results and Challenges

The PINN model demonstrated promising results, achieving high accuracy in option pricing when compared to analytical Black-Scholes solutions. However, I encountered some limitations in the initial implementation:

  • Fixed Parameters: The model was trained with specific values for volatility, maturity, and other constants, limiting its adaptability to varying market scenarios.
  • Generalization: The trained PINN couldn't automatically adapt to new parameter values without retraining, restricting its practical applicability.

Future Improvements

To address these challenges and enhance the model's capabilities, future work could focus on:

  • Parameter Incorporation: Modifying the PINN architecture to accept volatility, maturity, and other relevant parameters as inputs, allowing for more flexible and generalizable option pricing.
  • Multi-task Learning: Training the PINN on a diverse range of market conditions and option types simultaneously, enabling it to learn a more robust and general solution to the Black-Scholes PDE.
  • Adaptive Training Strategies: Implementing training regimes that gradually introduce varying market parameters, helping the PINN build a more comprehensive understanding of the underlying financial dynamics.
  • Extended Financial Models: Expanding the approach to handle more complex option types and multi-asset scenarios, potentially incorporating stochastic volatility or jump-diffusion models.
